Flood Records

Flooding is no longer a rare or isolated event in the UK. It damages homes, disrupts transport networks, and places growing pressure on local communities.

Between 2016 and 2019, floods caused an estimated £708 million in economic damage across England and Wales (Environment Agency, 2021), while around 5.2 million properties in England are currently at risk from flooding from rivers, the sea, or surface water (Environment Agency, 2020).

Flood risk is unevenly distributed across the United Kingdom and varies according to geography, drainage systems, coastal exposure and patterns of urban development (Environment Agency, 2020; Defra, 2021). To explore these regional contrasts, this dashboard focuses on four selected UK cities: Hull, York, Great Yarmouth and Greater London. Each city reflects a distinct flood-risk profile.

Market Context

Flood Risk × Housing Markets

Where environmental risk meets local market forces — four cities, four stories.

How Flood Risk Shapes Housing Prices

Flood risk is not only an environmental issue — it can also shape housing values.

Across England, homes exposed to flood risk were found to sell for around 8% less than comparable properties in safer locations, with discounts rising above 30% for the highest-risk homes (Bayes Business School, 2023). The effect is not limited to homes that have already flooded. Prices tend to fall gradually as estimated flood risk increases, suggesting that buyers are responding to future risk as well as past events. Sellers appear to recognise this too: they also tend to list properties at lower prices, and lower asking prices in higher-risk areas indicate that flood exposure is increasingly understood as part of the market.

But does this happen everywhere in the same way?

  • Hull

    Hull presents a different pattern. Unlike London, Hull shows a weaker pricing premium in higher-risk flood zones. Across much of the period, homes in Zone 3 remain priced below properties in medium-risk or no-flood areas, while price growth appears more moderate over time.

    This reflects Hull's distinct flood profile: the city is widely recognised for surface water flood risk, driven by low-lying topography, drainage pressure and limited natural outflow to the sea (Environment Agency, 2008). During the 2007 floods, more than 8,000 homes and 1,300 businesses were affected, making it one of the most significant urban flood events in recent UK history (Hull City Council, 2008).

    Therefore, the flatter trend in high-risk areas may suggest that, in lower-demand housing markets, flood exposure can place a more visible constraint on long-term price growth.

  • York

    York shows a more balanced pattern. Compared with London and Hull, price differences between flood zones in York appear narrower over time. Homes in higher-risk areas do not show a persistent discount, with all three flood-zone categories following a broadly similar upward trend across the period.

    This may reflect York's distinctive urban geography: the city has a long history of river flooding linked to the Rivers Ouse and Foss, yet many flood-prone areas are also close to the historic centre, established neighbourhoods and highly desirable residential locations (City of York Council, 2021).

    In this context, environmental risk may be offset by central accessibility and limited housing supply. As the chart suggests, flood exposure in York does not translate into a clear long-term price penalty, with local housing demand remaining an important counterweight.

  • Great Yarmouth

    Great Yarmouth shows a clearer flood-risk divide. Unlike London and York, homes outside designated flood zones remain consistently more expensive than properties in medium- or high-risk areas across most of the period.

    This may reflect Great Yarmouth's coastal flood profile: the town is particularly exposed to tidal surge risk and sea-level pressure due to its low-lying coastline and proximity to the North Sea, making flood exposure a more prominent local concern (Environment Agency, 2014).

    In this context, environmental risk may carry greater weight in buyer decisions, especially in a smaller and less supply-constrained housing market.

  • Greater London

    London offers a striking exception. Although flood risk is often associated with lower property values, homes located in higher-risk flood zones remain among the most expensive in the country.

    This is especially visible along the River Thames, where many flood-prone areas also benefit from strong demand, central locations and premium riverside developments. In these neighbourhoods, the value of accessibility, prestige and waterfront living can outweigh concerns over flood exposure.

    Therefore, the upward trend shown across all flood zones — with Zone 3 consistently recording the highest prices over time — suggests that flood risk does not always reduce prices directly.

Insights

Conclusion & Summary

Reflections, key findings, and what these recorded flood patterns mean for resilient urban planning.

Flood risk does not affect housing prices in a uniform way, but instead varies across different urban contexts. While it may constrain price growth in some areas, its impact can be offset elsewhere by strong housing demand, central location advantages, scenic waterfront value, or limited supply.

Looking ahead, climate change is likely to intensify these dynamics. Beyond areas primarily exposed to periodic and more manageable flooding, and beyond global cities such as London where amenity and location can partially offset risk, unprecedented extreme-weather flooding may significantly increase the vulnerability of residents living within flood-prone zones. As a result, the relationship between environmental risk and housing value may become more uneven, revealing wider differences in urban resilience, adaptive capacity, and long-term market confidence.

In this context, a city's ability to withstand and adapt to climate risk may become an increasingly important measure of urban value. The deeper question is whether this resilience can be distributed equitably, or whether climate adaptation will become another layer of urban inequality...
