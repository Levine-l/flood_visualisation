Background

Flooding is no longer a rare or isolated event in the UK. It damages homes, disrupts transport networks, and places growing pressure on local communities.

Between 2016 and 2019, floods caused an estimated £708 million in economic damage across England and Wales (Environment Agency, 2021), while around 5.2 million properties in England are currently at risk from flooding from rivers, the sea, or surface water (Environment Agency, 2020).

But flood risk is not evenly distributed. Different places face different types of flooding — from rivers and surface water to coastal exposure — and these local differences matter.

Notes

Flood risk is unevenly distributed across the United Kingdom and varies according to geography, drainage systems, coastal exposure and patterns of urban development (Environment Agency, 2020; Defra, 2021). To explore these regional contrasts, this dashboard focuses on four selected UK cities: Hull, York, Great Yarmouth and Greater London.

Each city reflects a distinct flood-risk profile. Hull is strongly associated with surface water flooding, particularly following the 2007 summer floods (Environment Agency, 2008). York has a long history of river flooding linked to the Rivers Ouse and Foss (City of York Council, 2021). Great Yarmouth remains vulnerable to coastal surge events due to its low-lying coastline (Environment Agency, 2014). Greater London faces multiple overlapping pressures including fluvial, surface water and tidal flood risks (Greater London Authority, 2022).

By comparing recorded flood events over time, this visualisation highlights how flooding patterns differ across seasons and locations, demonstrating the diverse and evolving nature of urban flood risk in the UK.

Interpretation: These counts represent distinct recorded flood events intersecting each city boundary — not flooded area, damage, or risk intensity. Larger areas such as Greater London may naturally contain more recorded events.
